## AFSL Responsible Manager Nominations

H1: **AFSL Responsible Manager Nominations**

Sub-sections:
- AFSL Responsible Managers and Key Persons
- AFSL Variations

**Service:** Grace Advisory assists AFSL holders with "nomination and/or termination of their Responsible Managers ('RMs'), Key Persons and variation of their AFSL conditions."

**RM definition:** Someone meeting knowledge & skills requirements under RG 105 to maintain authorisations granted to an AFSL holder. All RMs collectively must cover organisational competency requirements.

**Key Person requirements:**
- Written notice to ASIC required within 5 business days when a key person departs.
- Notice must include departure date and replacement details.
- If no replacement exists, licensees must explain why.
- Replacement requires AFSL licence variation, not just nomination.

**AFSL Variations:** Businesses expanding services or changing direction may need new authorisations, requiring variation applications that update business descriptions and organisational competence documentation.

### Blog post 1 — Tranche 2 Compliance Explained: What Has Changed Under AUSTRAC Reform?

**Intro:**
- One of Australia's biggest regulatory changes in decades is about to begin. There has been an update to the Anti-Money Laundering and Counter-Terrorism Financing (AML/CTF) Amendment Act.
- Thousands of Australian companies will have to comply starting on 1 July 2026. AML regulations did not previously apply to these companies. They have a wide range of new responsibilities to fulfil.
- The Tranche 2 reforms are the name given to these modifications. This impacts those who work as lawyers, accountants, real estate brokers, or trust providers.

**What Are the AML/CTF Reforms and Why Now?**
Australia has had an AML/CTF system in existence since 2006, but the framework was never applied to some high-risk professional and service sectors for almost twenty years. These industries are referred to as Tranche 2 enterprises — attorneys, accountants, real estate experts, and precious metal/stone traders. The Financial Action Task Force (FATF) consistently criticised their exclusion. FATF establishes international guidelines to combat money laundering and the funding of terrorism. Australia ran the risk of receiving a formal FATF non-compliance rating in the absence of reform.

**Who Is Captured by the Tranche 2 Reforms?**
From 1 July 2026, the following sectors will be regulated under Australian AML/CTF laws for the first time:
- Legal professionals (lawyers, conveyancers, notaries)
- Accountants and accounting firms
- Real estate agents (for property sales, purchases and rentals involving significant funds)
- Dealers in precious metals and gemstones
- Trust and company service providers

According to AUSTRAC, the Tranche 2 amendments will subject almost 90,000 new companies to AML supervision.

**What Changes Under the Tranche 2 AML Reforms?**

- **Expanded Definition of Virtual Assets** — "digital currency" is superseded by the broader concept of "virtual assets," widening the compliance framework to additional crypto-linked actions.
- **Updated Customer Due Diligence (CDD) Obligations** — a modernised, risk-based method introduced. Companies must identify and validate clients, understand the nature and intent of the relationship, and use enhanced vigilance for higher risks.
- **New Reporting and Value Transfer Rules** — updated regulations for the reporting and monitoring of foreign value transfers. International Value Transfer Service (IVTS) requirements are being phased in, with some entities delaying full implementation until 2029.
- **Repeal of the Financial Transaction Reports Act 1988** — all AML/CTF regulations combined into a single, cohesive framework with the abolition of the antiquated FTRA 1988.
- **Stronger Governance Obligations** — senior management and board members are specifically in charge of ensuring AML/CTF threats are recognised and handled throughout the company.

**Key Dates to Know:**

| Milestone | Date |
|---|---|
| AUSTRAC enrolment opens for new entities | 31 March 2026 |
| AML/CTF compliance obligations commence | 1 July 2026 |
| Enrolment deadline for new entities | 29 July 2026 |
| Compliance Officer notification to AUSTRAC | 30 May 2026 |
| Updated CDD (for existing reporting entities) | 30 March 2029 |

**What Does an AML/CTF Compliance Program Need to Include?**
Under the reformed Act, every regulated business must implement and maintain a documented AML/CTF compliance program — not one-size-fits-all; it must be tailored to the specific risks and operating model of your business.

A strong AML compliance program typically consists of:
1. **Evaluation of Business-Wide Risk** — risk assessment across clients, products, services, delivery methods and geographic exposure.
2. **Policies and Procedures for Risk Mitigation** — documented controls including transaction monitoring, customer screening, escalation protocols.
3. **Customer Due Diligence (CDD) Systems** — including enhanced due diligence for higher-risk interactions (high-value transactions, PEPs).
4. **Continuous Monitoring Systems** — track customer behaviour and transactions over time and flag anomalies.
5. **Suspicious Matter Reporting (SMR)** — clear protocols to promptly report suspicious behaviour to AUSTRAC.
6. **Record-keeping** — keep customer and transaction records for at least seven years.
7. **Staff Training** — appropriate AML/CTF training; awareness of warning signs, reporting requirements and risk-based approach.
8. **Designated AML/CTF Compliance Officer** — companies have until 30 May 2026 to designate and notify AUSTRAC.

**Steps to Prepare Before 1 July 2026:**
1. Confirm whether you are a reporting entity.
2. Enrol with AUSTRAC (between 31 March and 29 July 2026; enrol early).
3. Evaluate the risks facing the entire company.
4. Create a customised program based on your risk tolerance (AUSTRAC's Program Starter Kits can help newly regulated businesses).
5. Put CDD processes into practice (sanctions and PEP screening).
6. Educate your team (scenario-based training beats handing out policy texts).
7. Designate a compliance officer (deadline 30 May 2026).
8. Plan for your independent review — bake it into your compliance planning.

### Blog post 2 — Complete Guide to Applying for an AFSL in Australia (Step-by-Step)

**What Is An AFSL Licence?** Permits businesses to conduct financial product advice, financial product dealing, portfolio management, and other financial services in Australia.

**Who Needs an AFSL?** Businesses providing financial services including advice, product dealing, managed investment schemes, custody services, or trustee company services.

**Eight-Step Application Process:**
1. Identify Services — define which financial services you'll provide.
2. Check Requirements — verify organisational competence, financial capacity, risk management.
3. Appoint Responsible Managers — select experienced managers to ensure compliance.
4. Establish Compliance Systems — policies, procedures, training programs.
5. Prepare Documentation — business descriptions, financials, policies, manager credentials.
6. Submit Application — file through ASIC's eLicensing portal.
7. Respond to Queries — address regulator questions promptly.
8. Implementation — deploy compliance systems after approval.

**Ongoing Compliance Requirements:** regular reporting and record-keeping, staff training and competency updates, risk management and internal audits, client disclosure and transparency.

**Common Application Issues:** documentation difficulties, compliance knowledge gaps, finding suitable managers, slow regulatory responses.

**FAQ Highlights:**
- *Timeline:* ASIC targets 120 days; typically 4–8 months total.
- *Cost:* ASIC fees range $3,721–$7,537; total costs often $10,000–$60,000+.
- *Documents Required:* business model, financials, compliance frameworks, manager qualifications, organisational structure.
